How Is The Internet Changing Attention?

A deep dive into the complicated reality of the Internet for PSYCH 532: Psychological Effects of the Internet

Josh Young

Few questions in psychology feel more personally urgent right now than this one: is the internet changing our attention, and if so, how? We've all probably heard a different answer to this question from news articles, friends, parents, and studies. The answer is extremely complicated. Which brings me to the goal of this project: to take the question seriously and answer it honestly, by going beyond the surface level responses.

This project is structured as a "deep dive" into Unit 9 of PSYCH 532: Psychological Effects of the Internet at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, with the goal being to examine what research actually says about attention and the internet, rather than just what the headlines claim.

On one side, there is a real and widely shared concern that something has changed with our attention. Writers like Nicholas Carr have argued that online life shortens attention spans and makes sustained, deep thinking harder (Losowski, 2012; Muhunthan, 2013). In a 2012 Pew survey, nearly 90% of teachers reported that students could not pay attention the way they used to (Willingham, 2015). Anecdotally, the structure of the internet itself, with its endless links, notifications, and rapidly shifting content, does seem designed to pull our attention in a dozen directions at once.

On the other side, researchers have pushed back against these claims. The evidence that attention spans are shortening is surprisingly thin, with some studies finding that our attention spans are actually pretty much the same as they were 50 years ago (Willingham, 2015). Attention is not a single fixed resource that drains away with use; the brain is built to respond to change, and the internet may simply be a new context in which that adaptability plays out (Stafford, 2012).

What both sides agree on is that attention is not simple. This project is meant to work through the PSYCH 532 course materials on attention and bring in additional sources and arguments to push the conversation further. Rather than devivering a verdict, I want to lay out both views clearly and honestly, and then let readers draw their own conclusions. Because, as the research shows, the most interesting questions about attention and the internet are not the ones with easy answers.
