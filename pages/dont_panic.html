Dont Panic: What Research Shows

Pushing Back On the Fears

← Home

The concerns raised on the previous page are real, but they do not tell the whole story. A substantial body of research, much of it covered in PSYCH 532, pushes back on the most alarming claims and asks us to be more careful about how we interpret the evidence. This does not mean the Internet has no effect on attention, but it means the effect is much more complicated and contested than headlines often suggest. In this section, I will take you through the research laid down by scholars who argue that the Internet is not necessarily harming attention, and how in some cases it may even be enhancing it.

The Brain Changes, But That Is Normal

One of the most important things to understand about the debate about the internet is that brain change is not the same thing as brain damage. Stafford (2012) points out that everything we do changes our brains, from drinking tea to learning an instrument, and the Internet is no different. The relevant question is not whether the Internet changes our brains, but whether those changes are harmful to development, cognition, or attention. With that, the evidence is far less damning than many people assume.

Kane (2017) makes a similar point, noting that headlines claiming the Internet is permanently rewiring our brains are technically true but deeply misleading, because the brain is always rewiring in response to experiences. The more interesting scientific question is what specific skills Internet use might be strengthening or weakening, rather than whether change is happening at all. Framing ordinary neuroplasticity as a crisis obscures more than it reveals (Kane, 2017).

Attention Spans Are Not Actually Shrinking

One of the most persistent claims in the public conversation is that the Internet is literally shrinking our attention spans. Have you ever heard that the average American attention span is smaller than that of a goldfish? That is a myth! McGinty (2017) traces this statistic back to a 2015 Microsoft Canada report, which itself cites a website called Statistic Brain as its source. When journalists and researchers contacted the organizations listed as Statistic Brain's sources, including the National Center for Biotechnology Information and the Associated Press, none of them could locate any research that backed up the numbers (McGinty, 2017).

The goldfish comparison turns out to be a complete fabrication. Professor Felicity Huntingford, a researcher at the University of Glasgow who studies fish behavior explains that goldfish are actually used as a model for studying learning and memory precisely because they are capable of both (Maybin, 2017). The eight second figure has no scientific backing, and experts say that it is a fabricated number.

What the actual research shows is a lot more stable. Willingham (2015) argues that laboratory measures of attentional capacity have remained mostly consistent over decades, and psychologist Edward Vogel, who has been measuring college students for twenty years, describes performance as remarkably stable across the decades. (McGinty, 2017). Scientists measure attention span in two ways: how much we can hold in our mind and how well we sustain focus. Neither measure shows consistent decline (Willingham, 2015).

Willingham (2015) does offer one important fact. While our ability to focus may not have diminished, our desire to focus on one thing may have, as the Internet raises the bar for what can "earn" our attention. This is a real effect, but it is a motivational one rather than a neurological one

Brief Distractions Can Actually Help

The science also complicates the idea that any departure from sustained focus is harmful. Research from the University of Illinois found that brief mental diversions during long tasks actually helped participants maintain their performance over time, while those who worked continuously without breaks showed a steady decline in focus over time (Lleras, 2011). The explanation offered is that the brain habituates to constant stimulation, and a short redirect allows attention to be reactivated rather than faded into the background.

This finding has very real implications for how we interpret Internet use. If brief diversions preserve focus over time then checking a different tab during a long task or looking at that notification may not be the cognitive failure that it is often seen as. The problem, which ScienceDaily (2011) points out, is likely not the distraction itself but when the distractions are too frequent, which can lead to a loss of focus.

Attention May Be Improving In Certain Generations

Maybe the most counterintuitive finding in the literature comes from Andrzejewski et al. (2024), who conducted a cross-temporal meta analysis of attention test scores across 287 samples from 32 countries between 1990 and 2021. Rather than finding a generational decline in attention, they found moderate positive gains in concentration performance in adults over that period, a result they describe as a Flynn Effect. This is the opposite of what the panic narrative would predict, and it covers exactly the decades during which Internet use rose dramatically.

The finding does come with some important limitations. The gains were observed in adults but not children, and the authors note that children showed increases in errors and processing speed suggesting more impulsivity over time (Andrzejewski et al., 2024). But the adult result alone is a significant challenge to any simple story about the Internet degrading attention across the board.

Training Can Help, But Only So Far

Finally, research on multitasking training offers a more grounded picture of what internet-style cognitive demands actually do to us. Bender et al. (2017) found that intensive dual-task training produced substantial improvements in multitasking performance specifically, but those gains did not transfer to other untrained attention tasks. This suggests that the brain adapts to whatever it practices, but that adaptation is narrow rather than broad.

This is both reassuring and sobering because it means that heavy internet use is unlikely to be causing widespread, generalized attentional collapse. But it also means that getting better at navigating a fast-paced digital environment does not automatically make you better at the kind of deep, sustained focus that reading a book or writing a paper demands (Bender et al., 2017). The internet may be training a specific cognitive style, one that is genuinely useful in some contexts and genuinely limiting in others.
