What Is Attention?

Before examining whether the internet affects attention, it is necessary to define what attention is. Attention is the cognitive process of selectively concentrating on information while filtering out competing stimuli. Psychologists typically distinguish several subtypes:

  • Selective attention: focusing on one stimulus while filtering out others.
  • Sustained attention: maintaining focus on a task over an extended period of time.
  • Divided attention: splitting cognitive resources between multiple tasks simultaneously.
  • Executive attention: managing competing demands and resolving conflicts between responses.

These distinctions carry important implications for how researchers interpret findings. Claims that the internet is shortening attention spans often conflate these categories without specifying which attentional system is under examination. Ophir, Nass, and Wagner (2009) found that heavy media multitaskers showed reduced performance on tasks requiring the suppression of irrelevant stimuli, which reflects a deficit in selective attention specifically. Willingham (2015), however, argues that laboratory measures of overall attentional capacity have remained largely stable over decades. Specifying which type of attention is affected is therefore essential to evaluating any empirical claim about the internet's cognitive effects.

A Brief History of Distraction

Concerns about new media degrading cognitive ability are not new. As Muhunthan (2013) documents, Socrates warned that writing would erode memory, the printing press was condemned as spiritually corrupting, and Victorian commentators feared that trains and telegraphs would overwhelm the nervous system. Radio, cinema, and television each provoked comparable anxieties in their time, few of which materialized as predicted.

Tom Stafford (2012) situates contemporary internet anxiety within this same tradition, noting that because neuroplasticity means every experience changes the brain, the relevant question is not whether the internet changes cognition but whether those changes constitute harm. Neil Postman's Amusing Ourselves to Death (1985) raised analogous concerns about television decades before smartphones existed, suggesting a recurring pattern in how societies respond to new media. This historical perspective does not resolve the empirical question, but it does establish that the burden of proof falls on those claiming the internet represents a categorically unprecedented threat to cognition.

The Internet as a New Kind of Medium

Unlike passive media such as television or radio, the internet is interactive, always available, and in its dominant commercial forms, algorithmically optimized for sustained engagement. Social media feeds, infinite scroll, push notifications, and hyperlink navigation represent design patterns that systematically compete for attentional resources in ways that earlier media did not.

Daniel Graham (2021) argues that the internet exploits human attentional vulnerabilities through unpredictable reward delivery, near-zero switching costs, and continuous novelty. These features activate the same bottom-up attentional capture mechanisms that evolved to detect threat and opportunity in the physical environment. Klosowski (2012) offers a counterpoint, noting that distraction-seeking behavior predates the internet and that the evidence for uniquely internet-caused attentional harm remains methodologically limited. Both perspectives agree that the internet's design is structurally distinct from prior media. They disagree on whether that distinction produces lasting cognitive damage or simply represents a new context for attention management that users can learn to navigate.
