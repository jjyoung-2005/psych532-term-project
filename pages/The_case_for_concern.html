The Case for Concern

Why Researchers and Clinicians Are Starting to Worry

← Home

Especially in the last decade, a growing body of evidence is making it harder to dismiss concerns about the Internet's effects on attention in adults, and more concerningly, in children. Researchers across psychology and neurology have documented that the relationship between Internet use and attention may be more troubling than we want to admit. In this section, we will explore the views of researchers who argue that the Internet is harming attention, and the evidence they use to support their claims.

The Clinical View

Bhat (2017), a licensed clinical psychologist, observes that children today are exposed to a constant stream of digital stimulation, and that this level of input may be training young brains to expect a much higher level of reward than other activities. When those same brains are asked to focus on slower, less immediately rewarding rasks like rading or class activites, the results can look alarmingly like Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). Bhat argues that ADHD diagnoses among children has increased by almost 35% since 2003 raising the question of whether some of that is because of the Internet.

Tarawneh (2020), a neurologist at Ohio State University, argues that because Internet use so often involves rapid switching between stimuli,, tabs, and notifications, it may strengthen task-switching at the expense of focus. The result, she suggests, is that heavy Internet users may become a lot more distractable, not because their attention span has changed, but because they have become used to constant novelty that the Internet provides. This makes ordinary single-task focus feel unrewarding (Tarawneh, 2020).

What the Research Is Finding

Using the Stroop test as a measure of attentional control, Mihai and Rebeca-Isabela (2024) found a significant decrease in the ability to maintain attention among adults who identified as heavy Internet users. Their study consisted of 60 participants and found that excessive Internet use produced measurable changes in attention even without increasing the number of stimuli presented. While the authors acknowledge that research in this area remains limited, they argue that the results are consistent enough to warrant further investigation and public awareness efforts.

Poles (2025) reviewed the literature on social media and attention specifically among young adults, finding a consistent negative correlation between heavy social media use and sustained attention spans, working memory, and cognitive control. Additionally, Poles identifies the type of content as a meaningful variable, as users who engage primarily with short-form platforms like TikTok, Instagram, and Switter show worse attentional outcomes than users who engage with goal oriented platforms. This suggests that the concern is not simply about screen time, but about the specific features of certain kinds of Internet use.

The Strongest Evidence Yet

Probably the strongest evidence comes from a large randomized controlled trial by Castelo et al. (2025), published in PNAS Nexus, which used an experimental design to test whether improvements in attention. Participants who had mobile Internet blocked on their smartphones for two weeks showed significant improvements in sustained attention, as well as mental wellbeing. Castelo et al. (2025) describe the strength of the attention improvement as comparable to the difference associated with being 10 years younger, and found that 91% of participants improved on at least one of the three outcomes measured.

This seems to be causal evidence, not correlational. The researchers were not simply observing that people who use the internet less happen to have better attention, they were showing that removing internet access produced measurable attentional gains in the same people over time. That is a meaningfully stronger claim, and one that the reassurance-focused literature has not yet fully grappled with (Castelo et al., 2025).

Is the Panic Justified?

We should not panic. The evidence here is real and worth taking seriously, but many of the studies very recent, have small sample sized, and have not yet been replicated. The mechanisms linking Internet use to attention are still debated, and not every study finds the same effects. The point of this section is not to declare a winner of this debate, but to show that the concern is no longer just hysteria. However, the next section's reassurances should be considered equally.
